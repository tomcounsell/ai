# Full catalog of AI-writing tells

Adapted from Wikipedia's [Signs of AI writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing) and generalized for business content: documents, presentations, emails, chat messages, posts, and web copy.

Two standing caveats:

1. **Vocabulary lists drift.** The word clusters below are era-specific (largely 2023–2025 models). Treat them as illustrations of the *kind* of word to notice — abstract, grand, interchangeable — not a fixed blocklist. New models grow new tics; the structural patterns age better than the word lists.
2. **Every tell is a signal, not a verdict.** Humans write all of these sometimes. The finding is a *cluster* or a *habit* — the same construction three times in a page — judged as a human copy editor would.

---

## 1. Vocabulary

**Slop words.** Abstract, grand, and swappable without changing meaning:

> delve, tapestry, testament, underscore, pivotal, crucial, vital, intricate, robust, seamless, leverage, boasts, foster, elevate, empower, unlock, harness, navigate (metaphorically), landscape, realm, journey, ecosystem, synergy, holistic, comprehensive, vibrant, dynamic, cutting-edge, game-changing, transformative, invaluable, myriad, plethora

*Fix:* the plain word ("use" for "leverage," "shows" for "underscores") or deletion.

**Slop phrases.**

> "it's worth noting that," "it's important to remember," "in today's fast-paced world," "in the ever-evolving landscape of," "at the end of the day," "when it comes to," "a wide range of," "plays a key role in," "is a testament to," "stands as a," "continues to captivate," "rich cultural heritage," "natural beauty"

*Fix:* almost always deletable without loss.

**Excessive lexical variation.** Calling the same thing by a new synonym in every sentence (the app → the platform → the solution → the tool) to avoid repetition. Humans repeat the noun; readers rely on it. *Fix:* pick one term and reuse it.

## 2. Sentence patterns

**Negative parallelism.** "Not just X, but Y." "It's not about X — it's about Y." "X isn't merely Y; it's Z." Rhetorically challenges a misconception nobody held. *Fix:* state the true thing directly.

> Before: "This isn't just a dashboard — it's a window into your entire operation."
> After: "The dashboard shows every session, health check, and reflection in one place."

**Rule of three.** Triads of adjectives ("fast, reliable, and scalable"), clauses, examples, or section counts, applied compulsively for artificial completeness. *Fix:* keep the strongest item; two or four also work.

**Copula avoidance.** "Serves as," "functions as," "stands as," "acts as," "features," "offers" where "is" or "has" is meant.

> Before: "The script serves as the entry point and features automatic retry."
> After: "The script is the entry point and retries automatically."

**Trailing "-ing" significance clauses.** A concrete statement with a vague commentary clause bolted on: ", highlighting the importance of observability," ", ensuring a seamless experience," ", creating a vibrant community." *Fix:* delete the clause; if the point matters, give it its own sentence with actual content.

**Hedge-hype mix.** "Could potentially," "truly unique," "very essential," stacked intensifiers next to stacked qualifiers. *Fix:* one modifier max, and only if it earns its place. Keep hedges that carry real uncertainty ("this may fail under load" is information, not slop).

**Uniform rhythm.** Every sentence 15–25 words, subject-verb-object, often with a medial em dash; every paragraph exactly three sentences. Reads as a metronome. *Fix:* vary length aggressively — a four-word sentence after two long ones is a very human move.

## 3. Tone and content

**Inflated significance.** Mundane details framed as historic: "marks a pivotal moment," "represents a significant milestone," "reflects a broader shift," "left an indelible mark," "plays a vital role in shaping." *Fix:* state the concrete stake or cut. If the release genuinely matters, say what changes for whom.

**Promotional register.** Travel-brochure or sales-page adjectives in what should be neutral prose: "vibrant," "nestled," "in the heart of," "showcasing," "renowned." Common when summarizing a company or product. *Fix:* facts only; let the reader be impressed.

**Weasel attribution.** "Experts agree," "many believe," "industry reports suggest," "observers note," "it is widely recognized." *Fix:* name the source, or own the claim in first person, or cut it.

**Editorializing.** The writer's judgment smuggled into description: "impressively," "notably," "unfortunately," "of course." *Fix:* in neutral documents, cut; in opinionated pieces, make the opinion explicit and owned instead of adverbial.

**Superficial analysis.** Commentary that sounds analytical but says nothing: "contributing to socio-economic development," "fostering innovation across the sector." *Fix:* replace with the specific mechanism or number, or cut.

**Sycophantic and filler openers (messages).** "Great question!," "I hope this email finds you well," "Thanks so much for reaching out!," restating the question back before answering. *Fix:* answer first. Warmth is fine; formula is the tell.

**Reflexive closers (messages).** "Let me know if you have any questions!," "Happy to help!," "Hope this helps!" appended to everything. *Fix:* close with the next concrete step, or just stop.

## 4. Structure

**Formulaic scaffolding.** An intro that previews the sections, sections that each open with a mini-thesis and close with a mini-summary, and a conclusion that restates the intro. The piece spends a third of its length describing itself. *Fix:* cut previews and recaps; trust the headings.

**"Challenges and future prospects" shape.** "Despite its [praise], [subject] faces challenges such as [generic list]. Looking ahead, [speculation]." A balanced-sounding section with no information. *Fix:* cut, or replace with the one real risk and the one real plan.

**Everything-in-sections disease.** A three-paragraph answer forced into five headed sections with bold lead-ins. *Fix:* prose. Headings are for documents long enough to navigate.

**Symmetric everything.** Every section the same length, every list the same item count, every heading the same grammatical shape. *Fix:* let content determine size.

**Summary tables of things that aren't data.** A two-column table restating the prose ("Aspect / Description"). *Fix:* cut the table or cut the prose — never both.

## 5. Formatting

**Bold overuse.** Mechanical emphasis on **key phrases** in every paragraph, "key takeaways" styling, bolded lead-ins on every bullet. *Fix:* bold only what a scanning reader must not miss — usually nothing.

**`**Header:** description` bullet walls.** Long runs of `- **Thing:** explanation` that are really paragraphs wearing a list costume. *Fix:* prose, or a real list with parallel, short items.

**Emoji as structure.** 🚀 as section markers, ✅/❌ as list bullets, decorative emoji in headings. *Fix:* remove unless the medium genuinely calls for it (a celebratory chat message ≠ a design doc).

**Em-dash overuse.** Em dashes substituting for commas, colons, parentheses, and periods — several per paragraph — until the punctuation blurs. *Fix:* keep the ones doing real appositive work; convert the rest.

**Title Case Headings** where the surrounding document uses sentence case, and Title Case on mid-sentence terms that aren't proper nouns ("our Media Coverage grew"). *Fix:* match house style; default sentence case.

**Gratuitous horizontal rules** before every heading. *Fix:* delete; the heading is the separator.

**Skipped heading levels** (H1 → H3) and inconsistent list markers (•, -, – mixed). *Fix:* normalize.

**Curly/straight quote inconsistency** — smart quotes in one paragraph, straight in the next — betrays pasted-in generated text. *Fix:* normalize to the document's convention.

## 6. Artifacts (always bugs — remove on sight)

- Leftover model citation markers: `oaicite`, `contentReference`, `turn0search0`, `[cite: 1]`, `[span_1]`, `grok_card`, `ppl-ai-file-upload`, stray dagger symbols
- Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers: "As of my last update," "as of [date], I don't have information on"
- Placeholder text: "This section would explore...," "[insert name]," "[citation needed]" outside Wikipedia
- Prompt echoes: "Certainly! Here's a revised version that...", "I've made the tone more professional"
- `utm_source` and other tracking parameters pasted into cited URLs
- Broken or fabricated references: URLs that 404, invalid DOIs/ISBNs, citations that don't support the claim they're attached to (spot-check any citation that looks too convenient)

## 7. What survives the pass untouched

- Facts, numbers, names, dates, quotes, prices, commitments
- Hedges carrying real uncertainty ("untested above 10k rows")
- The author's genuine stylistic habits — the context file (`.claude/skill-context/de-slop.md`) can whitelist them explicitly, and absent one, a tell used once with evident intent is not a finding
- Structure the medium requires: deck bullets, runbook steps, API tables
- Anything whose "fix" would change meaning. When removal would alter substance, flag it in the report instead of editing
